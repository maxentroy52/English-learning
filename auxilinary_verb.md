This is an excellent question that gets to the very heart of how English works. You're absolutely right that English has a rich system of verbs (transitive, intransitive, tenses, non-finites). So why add auxiliary verbs into the mix?

It's a common point of confusion. Let's break it down. You've hit on the core reason: **auxiliary verbs exist to do the heavy lifting that main verbs, by themselves, simply cannot do.**

Think of a main verb (like *eat, go, see, be, have*) as the engine of a car. The engine provides the core action or state. But an engine can't steer, signal, or play the radio. **Auxiliary verbs are the steering wheel, the turn signals, and the stereo system.** They add layers of grammatical meaning that a single verb form can't express on its own.

Let's define them and then explore *why* they are indispensable.

---

### What is an Auxiliary (Helping) Verb?

An auxiliary verb is a verb that "helps" another verb (the main verb) form a verb phrase. It does not carry the primary meaning of the action; instead, it adds grammatical information like:

- **Tense** (when)
- **Aspect** (how the action unfolds over time: ongoing, completed, repeated)
- **Mood** (the speaker's attitude: possibility, necessity, obligation)
- **Voice** (active or passive)
- **Negation** (making something negative)
- **Inversion** (forming a question)

There are two main categories:

1.  **Primary Auxiliaries:** *be, have, do*
2.  **Modal Auxiliaries:** *can, could, will, would, shall, should, may, might, must*

---

### Why We Need Them: The Problems Main Verbs Can't Solve Alone

You mentioned we have tenses (like past tense *walked*). That's true for simple statements. But what if you want to express more complex ideas?

#### 1. To Express **Aspect** (The "Shape" of an Action)

A simple past tense like *I walked* is a single, completed event. But what if you want to say:

- **The action was in progress at a specific time in the past?** (e.g., "I *was walking* home when I saw her.")
    - *Walked* alone can't do this. You need the auxiliary **be** + the present participle (*-ing* form) to create the **continuous aspect**.

- **The action was completed *before* another point in time?** (e.g., "I *had already eaten* when he arrived.")
    - *Ate* alone can't show this sequence. You need the auxiliary **have** + the past participle to create the **perfect aspect**.

You can even combine them: "I *have been waiting* for an hour." This uses two auxiliaries (*have* and *been*) to express both the perfect aspect (action completed relative to now) and the continuous aspect (ongoing action). Main verbs simply lack the grammatical structure to do this on their own.

#### 2. To Form **Questions** and **Negatives**

This is a huge one. In English, you can't just invert a main verb or put "not" after it in most cases.

- **Question:** You want to ask if someone walked.
    - ❌ *Walked you?* (Incorrect in modern English)
    - ✅ ***Did** you walk?* (You need the auxiliary **do**)

- **Negative:** You want to say you did not walk.
    - ❌ *I walked not.* (Archaic and incorrect for most verbs)
    - ✅ *I **did not** walk.* (You need the auxiliary **do**)

The only exception is with *be* as a main verb ("Are you happy?" "I am not tired") and with modals ("Can you swim?" "I cannot swim"). For all other main verbs, you are *grammatically required* to use the auxiliary **do** to form questions and negatives.

#### 3. To Express **Mood** (Possibility, Necessity, Permission)

This is the job of the **modal auxiliaries**. You can't express these nuanced meanings with the main verb alone.

- **Ability:** I **can** swim.
- **Possibility:** It **might** rain.
- **Necessity/Obligation:** You **must** leave.
- **Prediction:** It **will** snow.
- **Advice:** You **should** rest.

A main verb like *swim* or *rain* can't carry this meaning of the speaker's judgment or likelihood. Modals are the dedicated tool for this.

#### 4. To Form the **Passive Voice**

When the subject is acted upon rather than doing the action, you need the auxiliary **be** + the past participle.

- **Active:** The dog *bit* the man.
- **Passive:** The man **was bitten** by the dog.

You can't just change *bit* to show the passive. You need the auxiliary *was*.

---

### Why Not Just Use Tenses and Non-Finites?

You're correct that English has a rich verb system. But those tenses (like *walks, walked*) are limited to simple time reference. Non-finite verbs (infinitives like *to walk*, gerunds like *walking*) can't act as the main verb of a clause. They need a helping verb to complete the idea.

**Auxiliaries are the grammatical "bridge" that connects your core meaning (the main verb) to these complex grammatical functions.**

Consider a simple sentence with a non-finite verb:
- *He **can** swim.* (Modal auxiliary + bare infinitive)
- *He **is** swimming.* (Primary auxiliary + present participle)

Without the auxiliary (*can, is*), *swim* and *swimming* are not complete predicate verbs in a sentence.

### Summary Table

| Grammatical Need | Without Auxiliary | With Auxiliary | Auxiliary Used |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Ongoing action** | I work. (simple, habitual) | I **am working**. | **be** |
| **Completed action** | I work. | I **have worked**. | **have** |
| **Question** | You work? (informal, ambiguous) | **Do** you work? | **do** |
| **Negative** | I work not. (archaic) | I **do not** work. | **do** |
| **Possibility** | I go. (statement of fact) | I **might** go. | **might** (modal) |
| **Passive** | The cake ate. (makes no sense) | The cake **was eaten**. | **be** |
| **Emphasis** | I worked. | I **did** work! | **do** |

### Conclusion

Auxiliary verbs aren't redundant. They are the grammatical toolkit that allows English to be so precise. While main verbs provide the vast vocabulary of actions and states, **auxiliaries provide the essential grammatical structure** for time relationships (aspect), speaker attitude (mood), question formation, negation, and voice.

Think of it this way:
- **Main Verbs** = The **vocabulary** of action.
- **Auxiliary Verbs** = The **grammar** of action.

You need both. The main verb tells you *what* happened. The auxiliary tells you *when*, *how*, *if*, and from *what perspective*.

Excellent follow-up question! You've spotted a key point that often confuses learners—and you're absolutely correct.

**Yes, *be* can function in two completely different ways:**

1.  As an **auxiliary (helping) verb**
2.  As a **linking verb (copula)**

This dual nature is unique to *be* (and to a lesser extent *have*). Let's distinguish them clearly.

---

### 1. *Be* as an Auxiliary Verb

When *be* is an auxiliary, it **must** be followed by another verb. Its job is to help that main verb form a specific grammatical structure.

| Structure | Example | Role of *be* |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Continuous aspect** | She *is watching* TV. | Helps *watching* form the continuous tense |
| **Passive voice** | The cake *was eaten*. | Helps *eaten* form the passive voice |

In these sentences, *is* and *was* are not the main meaning—the action is *watching* and *eaten*. *Be* is purely grammatical.

**Key test:** If you can remove *be* and the remaining verb form cannot stand alone as a complete predicate, it's likely an auxiliary.

---

### 2. *Be* as a Linking Verb (Copula)

When *be* is a linking verb, it **is** the main verb. It connects the subject to a subject complement (an adjective, noun, or phrase that describes or identifies the subject).

| Structure | Example | Role of *be* |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Subject + Adjective** | You *are* beautiful. | Main verb linking subject *you* to adjective *beautiful* |
| **Subject + Noun** | She *is* a doctor. | Main verb linking subject *she* to noun phrase *a doctor* |
| **Subject + Prepositional Phrase** | They *are* in the garden. | Main verb linking subject *they* to location *in the garden* |

Here, *are* carries the core meaning of the sentence (existence, identity, or state). There is no other verb.

**Key test:** If *be* is the only verb in the clause and is followed by an adjective, noun, or prepositional phrase describing the subject, it's a linking verb.

---

### How to Tell Them Apart: A Simple Test

Look at the word that comes after *be*:

| After *be* | Function | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Present participle (-ing)** | Auxiliary (continuous) | He *is running*. |
| **Past participle (-ed / -en)** | Auxiliary (passive) | It *was broken*. |
| **Adjective (beautiful, tall, tired)** | Linking verb | She *is beautiful*. |
| **Noun phrase** | Linking verb | He *is a teacher*. |
| **Prepositional phrase** | Linking verb | They *are at home*. |

---

### A Special Case: *Be* Can Switch Roles

Sometimes the same verb form can appear in both roles depending on meaning:

> *She is cooking.*
- *Is* = auxiliary (helping *cooking* form continuous tense)
- Meaning: She is performing the action of cooking right now.

> *She is a cooking instructor.*
- *Is* = linking verb (main verb connecting *she* to the noun phrase *a cooking instructor*)
- Meaning: Her identity is a cooking instructor.

---

### What About *Have*?

You mentioned *have* earlier, and it's worth noting that *have* behaves similarly:

| Role | Example |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Auxiliary** (perfect aspect) | I *have seen* that movie. |
| **Main verb** (possession, obligation, etc.) | I *have* a car. / I *have to* go. |

So *be* and *have* are special—they can "wear two hats."

---

### Summary

| | **Auxiliary *be*** | **Linking *be*** |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Role** | Helps another verb | Is the main verb itself |
| **Followed by** | Present participle (-ing) or past participle | Adjective, noun, or prepositional phrase |
| **Example** | She *is sleeping*. | She *is sleepy*. |
| **Meaning** | Grammatical aspect/voice | State, identity, location |

Your observation is spot on: the same word *be* can function either as a helper or as the main verb. Context—specifically what follows it—tells you which role it's playing.

You've hit on one of the most subtle and fascinating areas of English grammar. Your instinct is absolutely correct: **while *could* and *would* historically originated as the past tenses of *can* and *will*, treating them simply as "past tense words" will lead to confusion.**

In modern English, *would* is a **modal auxiliary** with a range of uses that extend far beyond talking about the past. Let me walk you through when and how to use it.

---

## The "Past Tense" Myth—Why It's Misleading

You're right to question this. Look at these two sentences:

> *I **can** swim.* (present ability)
> *I **could** swim when I was five.* (past ability)

Here, *could* does function as the past of *can*. Similarly:

> *I **will** call you.* (present/future intention)
> *He said he **would** call me.* (past intention—reported speech)

So in these **reported speech** and **past ability** contexts, the "past tense" label holds true.

But consider this:

> *I **would** like a cup of tea.*

This is not about the past. It's a **polite request in the present**. So clearly, *would* has a life of its own.

---

## The Main Uses of *Would*

Let me break down the seven most common situations where *would* is the natural—or even the only—choice.

---

### 1. Polite Requests and Offers

This is one of the most frequent uses in everyday conversation. *Would* softens a request, making it more polite than *will*.

| Use | Example |
| :--- | :--- |
| Polite request | **Would** you open the window, please? |
| Polite offer | **Would** you like some coffee? |
| Expressing desire | I **would** like to visit Paris someday. |

**Compare:** *Will you open the window?* sounds more like a command. *Would* creates distance and politeness.

---

### 2. Hypotheticals, Conditionals, and Imaginary Situations

This is where *would* truly shines. When a situation is imagined, unreal, or dependent on a condition, *would* is required.

> *If I won the lottery, I **would** buy a house.*
> (I'm not imagining I will win—this is a hypothetical.)

> *What **would** you do if you were in his position?*

In these **second conditional** sentences, *would* signals that we are in the realm of imagination, not reality.

---

### 3. Past Habits (Repeated Actions)

When you want to describe a habit or repeated action in the past, *would* can be used—but only for actions, not states.

> *When I was a child, my grandfather **would** take me fishing every weekend.*

This is similar to *used to*, but there's a key difference:

| | *Used to* | *Would* |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Past actions** | ✅ *We used to go swimming.* | ✅ *We would go swimming.* |
| **Past states** | ✅ *I used to be shy.* | ❌ *I would be shy.* |

*Would* cannot describe past states—only repeated actions.

---

### 4. Reported Speech (The "True" Past Tense Use)

When reporting what someone said, *will* becomes *would*.

> Direct: *She said, "I **will** come."*
> Reported: *She said she **would** come.*

This follows the standard backshifting rule in indirect speech.

---

### 5. Unreal or Imagined Present/Future

Sometimes *would* expresses a wish or preference about the present or future that is not (or may not be) true.

> *I wish it **would** stop raining.*
> (It's raining now, and I want it to change.)

> *If only he **would** listen.*

This conveys a sense of desire mixed with frustration—the situation is not currently as you want it to be.

---

### 6. Polite Opinions and Softening Statements

*Would* can make an opinion or suggestion sound less direct and therefore more polite or diplomatic.

> *I **would** say you should wait a bit longer.*
> (Softer than: *You should wait.*)

> *That **would** be a mistake, in my opinion.*

---

### 7. Past Future (Future from a Past Perspective)

When talking about a future event from a point in the past, *would* is used.

> *He knew he **would** be late.*
> (At that past moment, the future event—being late—was still ahead.)

> *They decided they **would** move to London.*

This is essentially the past equivalent of *will*.

---

## A Quick Comparison: *Would* vs. *Could*

Since you mentioned *could*, here's how they differ:

| | *Would* | *Could* |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Polite request** | *Would you help me?* (willingness) | *Could you help me?* (ability/possibility) |
| **Hypothetical** | *I would go if I had time.* (intention) | *I could go if I had time.* (possibility) |
| **Past habit** | *We would play outside.* (action) | (Not used for habits) |

---

## Summary Table: When to Use *Would*

| Situation | Example |
| :--- | :--- |
| Polite request/offer | *Would you help? / Would you like tea?* |
| Hypothetical (second conditional) | *If I were you, I would wait.* |
| Past habit (action only) | *Grandma would tell us stories.* |
| Reported speech | *He said he would come.* |
| Wish about present/future | *I wish it would stop.* |
| Softening an opinion | *I would think that's a bad idea.* |
| Future from past perspective | *She knew she would succeed.* |

---

## A Final Thought

Your confusion is completely understandable. Many learners (and even native speakers) struggle with *would* because it wears so many hats. The key insight is this:

**Think of *would* not as a "past tense word," but as the **modal of distance**—distance from reality (hypotheticals), distance from directness (politeness), or distance in time (past habits and reported speech).**

Once you grasp that unifying idea—*would* creates a sense of remoteness, whether in time, reality, or directness—its many uses start to feel less like a list of exceptions and more like a coherent system.
